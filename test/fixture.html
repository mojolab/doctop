Heading 1

Over 44 per cent of people who are expected to vote in 2015 say they will be voting for a different party than they did in 2010, according to a new Times data analysis.

[Sankey with 2010 flows to 2015 for all movement out of parities]

Heading 1 the second

Using electoral roll data and YouGov polls of voter intention from October, The Times Data Team projects that there will be 13.4 million voters changing parties in the next election. Over half of this is due the collapse in Liberal Democrat support and the rise of Ukip, as reflected in the latest polls.

The analysis reveals a highly fluid situation, with the traditional mainstream parties losing large portions of their votes to smaller players, such as Ukip, the Greens and the SNP. More than one in eight do not yet know who they will vote for, and XXX say they do not currently intend to vote.

The level of voter turnover is far higher than in previous electoral cycles. Data from the British Election Study shows that in previous elections approximately 25% per cent of voters switched their vote. The study used a face-to-face interview rather than a remote interview, therefore the comparison is only approximate.

Sir David Butler, emeritus professor at Nuffield College Oxford and co-inventor of the “swingometer”, told Red Box: “I have been tracking elections since the 1950s and the volatility of voters is greater than ever before.”

He added: “I have a horrible feeling that something fundamental is happening. I have never felt so uncertain about predicting elections as I do now.”

Professor David Denver, author of Elections and Voters in Britain, said: “This level of voter turnover is very unusual - it’s much, much bigger than you would normally get.”

He added that in polls of voter intention, people often misreport how they voted in the last election: “People quite often forget how they voted five years ago, and they tend to say they voted in the last election how they are currently planning to vote in the next. They make themselves look more consistent - that’s what makes these figures even more remarkable.”

Experts say long-term patterns in voting behaviour and more recent changes in the political landscape are the reason 2015’s election will see such significant shifts.

Peter Kellner, president of YouGov, told Red Box: “In general, we are much less politically loyal, and much less partisan, than we used to be. One reason for this is the change in the country’s economic and social structure: life has become more atomised and it is no longer conducive to the mass organisation of political movements.”

He added: “There are two big factors in next year’s election: the collapse of Lib Dem support, and the rise of Ukip… This makes it inevitable that you’re going to have a much higher turnover than normal.”

The analysis shows that the only two parties expected to gain votes [have a net balance between votes gained and votes lost to other parties] are Ukip and the Greens.

Mr Kellner said that voters are moving to these parties partly to express dissatisfaction with the mainstream parties: “The protest vote is more significant than ever before, partly because of declining trust in politicians.”

He added: “The Liberal Democrats used to be the default protest vote, before they went into government. Now Ukip is taking that place and so are the Greens.”

[Sankey Ukip 2015]

Ukip is set to see the biggest growth, gaining 3.3 million voters from other parties. Eighty-six per cent of those who say they intend to vote Ukip have not voted Ukip in the past. Almost two million people who voted Tory in 2010 are predicted to turn to Ukip, making up 47 per cent of Ukip’s projected 2015 vote. The party is also expected have a base with 17 per cent from the Lib Dems and 15 from Labour.

This puts the party at 13.8 per cent of total vote share, compared to 3.1 in the last election.

Mr Kellner said Ukip is successfully tapping into the decline in public trust, “the belief that Britain’s going downhill”. He added: “Ukip’s vote is heavily tilted to older, working-class men, so  what I think is happening is that Ukip is appealing to people whose families traditionally voted Labour, who voted Tory themselves in the 1980s, with Thatcherism. These are people who the Labour party used to have, and ought to win back now.”

[Sankey Green 2015]

The Greens are expected to pick up nearly a million new voters, up from 286,000 in 2010. This takes them to a predicted 4.5 per cent of the vote share. Over 84 per cent of the party’s support in 2015 will come from those who previously voted for other parties.

The Greens are scaling up their ambitions to match, reportedly targeting a dozen seats at next year’s election. The party is viewed as enough of a threat by Labour for the party to charge Sadiq Khan, the shadow justice secretary, with seeing off a threat from the Greens. But the party represents a far greater threat to the Lib Dems. The analysis echoes previous YouGov research in finding that around half of the new Green voters are former Liberal Democrat supporters.

[Sankey Lib Dem 2010]

As Red Box reported last month, the Lib Dems are to expect a massive loss of voters. The party is to have a net loss of 5.4 million votes with 1.8 million going to Labour and 820,000 going to the Conservatives. They are only holding on to 22 per cent of their 2010 base in the next election.

Professor Denver said: “The Lib Dems have always had the lowest retention rate of the main parties, but this time it will be absolutely catastrophic.”

However favourable constituency boundaries and pockets of regional support will prevent annihilation for the Lib Dems, predicted Sir Butler. “Based on the polls, they should be getting between zero and five seats, but in fact they will get maybe 25 to 30 seats.” This would still see the party lose around half its current 56 MPs.

Labour and the Conservatives can also expect to lose vote share next year. This is part of a long-term fragmentation of the UK’s political scene: in the 1959 general election, Labour and the Conservatives between them took 96.1 per cent of the votes. By 2010, just over half a century later, this was down to 65 per cent.

“The Liberal Democrats mucked up the nice neat Labour-Tory battle, which had run from about 1935 to 1997 or thereabouts,” said Sir Butler. “Next time around Labour and the Tories combined won’t get more than 65 per cent. That means much more variability and much more unpredictability.”

Professor Denver said: “Neither of the main parties has exactly been a roaring success in office… Britain has slipped down the international rankings, and the country has changed without people being consulted. Part of what we are seeing is a general disaffection with politics and parties.”

[Sankey Cons 2010]

The Conservatives are expected to lose 2.4 million votes. This is one third of their 2010 base. Former Tory voters are particularly likely to say they will switch to Ukip. Professor Denver said: “David Cameron has gone all-out to decontaminate the brand, to make the Tories seem nice - that has meant doing un-Tory things, such as allowing same-sex marriage. That was a viable strategy when discontented voters had nowhere else to go, but now they have got somewhere to go.”

However, the Tories are also likely to benefit from the collapse of the Lib Dems: 9 per cent of those who say they intend to vote for the party in 2015 are switching from Nick Clegg’s party.

[Sankey Labour 2010]

Labour are expected to lose 300,000 votes in 2015. The party saw a 2 per cent drop in the polls between September and October.

Although they are now neck and neck with the Conservatives, Labour has the strongest hold on their 2010 votes, with 72 per cent retention. More than 20 per cent of those expected to vote for the party in 2010 are coming from the Lib Dems.

[Sankey Labour 2015]

In Scotland, the Scottish National Party is leading in the polls with nearly 34 per cent of the vote. The latest polls show they are grabbing 23 per cent of Labour’s 2010 supporters, 27 per cent of the Liberal Democrats and 30 per cent of the Greens.

 

[Sankey Don’t know 2015]

As of October, there are 3.8 million people who voted in 2010 but don’t know what they would vote if elections were to be held tomorrow. Mr Kellner said that many of these voters are likely to opt for the party they voted for in the 2010 election.

With the general election still six months away, the polls could still shift significantly. “Quite often you’ll see an upsurge in support for small parties mid-term, but that can fade quite rapidly as the general election approaches… There’s time for a correction to happen,” said Professor Denver. But he added: “I don’t think we will ever go back to simple two-party politics. People are too volatile and willing to try new things.”

Sir David Butler agreed that the more fragmented electoral landscape may be here to stay. “This is probably the way things will be for the foreseeable future. I can’t see it returning to some nice tidy Tory-Labour situation.”

The sample does not include voters in Northern Ireland. Only people who declared that they voted in 2010 and that they would vote if elections would be tomorrow were used in the analysis. The calculated intentions were combined with votes projections for the UK and extended to first time voters and voters in Northern Ireland.

FOR THE SANKEY: The 2010 vote shares are based on 2014 polls and do not include Northern Ireland, therefore there is a small difference with 2010 actual results. This is also due to the so-called ‘false recall’, when people don’t remember or don’t want to admit what they voted for in previous elections.
